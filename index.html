So, you've never heard of the Kinetic Dream? It's no wonder. This was a gathering of three young men who were all located in Huntsville, Alabama. They all had one thing in common, they all loved rock music. Being southern rebels, they didn't want to just be a cover band, Murphy (who was called Bob at that time) and "Stewart" became music and song writers and came up with a folk/rock sound that was different than anything out at the time. The time was 1981. MTV was fresh, the music on the radio was evolving from mostly black urban, punk to main stream pop. Murphy Gunnison was recently in the Navy and had gone absent without leave, unknown to the rest of the band, family and friends. He just was reaching out and grabbing at whatever came his way. At some point he met Michael McAnally, the soon to be "back beat" drummer for the group, and they struck up a brotherly friendship that has withstood the test of time to this day. Murphy had now found something he'd wanted as far back as he could remember, a friendship and an outlet for his music. He had put his Navy troubles in a dark place in his mind and just sort of forgot all about it and enjoyed what was to be the best year of his life. Mike McAnally was kind of a mellow guy who has a great sense of humor and rhythm. He was also nobody's fool. He could smell trouble from a distance and steered clear of it, or them, whenever possible. He was, and is, an extremely talented drummer and guitar player. On drums, he could pattern his style to the likes of the group "Rush's" Neil Pert, the Beatle's Ringo Starr and others, with amazing precision and accuracy. His demeanor could literally turn a funeral into a party. Donald Stewart was his momma a daddy's son. He was very loving and watchful over them. Not clear exactly if the group found him or he found the group. At any rate, the group was "the Kinetic Dream" with him as front man. He had a qwerky kind of voice (in a good way) that actually fit perfect and made the sound complete and very captivating. So, at this point it was "Stewart" - lead vocals, rhythm guitar. "Bob" - back vocals, rhythm guitar, some lead guitar and the bass guitar. Mike - drums, back vocals and rhythm guitar. And from time to time, Stewart's cousin Jeff Kendrick sat in on bass guitar. From here on, they played a couple parties and at a farm concert in the middle of no where. Not much of a tour at all, quite boring. They even traveled to Nashville to try to impress anyone on Music Row, and they kind-a-sort-a did. They traveled to Nashville with a *postal copyright copy of their original music. They drove to a recording studio next door to Capitol Records where Paul McCartney had been rumored to have frequently visited. The place was American Sound Records. A very modest looking place that looked more like a house than a business. They proudly walked in with their tape and told the gentleman who they were and handed him the tape. He unwrapped it, spooled it up on his reel-to-reel tape machine and hit play. Their hearts were pounding as it started playing the first song. It wasn't them. It was Don's father playing a slightly out of tune guitar and singing some old country songs. What made it worse, the tape was recorded in 8 desperate tracks, four forward and four reverse, and all of them were playing at the same time....a train wreck had occurred. The blood rushed out of their faces and they were devastated . The man at the studio said "y'all look like fine young men and I have no doubt you are good, just bring back the right tape later and we'll try again. That was the beginning of the end for the group. To make a short story shorter, the Navy had put the word out that they were trying to locate "Bob". When word got to Stewart, he punched Bob in the nose and Bob went back to the Navy and turned his self in. Due to his testimony and a key military witness, the charge of AWOL was dropped without punishment and he went on to be Honorably Discharged. Now, here it is 36 years later and life has happened and an opportunity knocks. Where will this road lead to? *A postal copyright is when you record the music and lyrics on a tape, wrap it up, address it to yourself and when you get it back, the cancellation stamp show that the media recorded was written and performed before that date

The Full Complexity of Making a Song with AI — Start to Finish

THE FULL COMPLEXITY OF MAKING A SONG WITH AI — START TO FINISH

1. 🧠 Concept & Vision Problems

  • Creative direction — You must define genre, mood, tempo, structure, influences, instrumentation, vocal style, and emotional intent before touching the AI.
  • Reference accuracy — AI misinterprets references (e.g., “psychedelic Beatles 1967” becomes “generic sitar pop”).
  • Style blending — Cross‑genre fusion (Beatles + Harrison + Shankar) requires extremely precise prompt engineering.
  • Vision drift — AI keeps pulling the song toward clichés or unrelated genres.
  • Emotional translation — AI doesn’t “feel” your intent; you must over‑specify emotional tone.

2. 🎛️ Prompt Engineering Challenges

  • Prompt specificity — Too vague = bland. Too detailed = chaotic.
  • Contradictory instructions — “Sparse but full,” “psychedelic but clean,” “heavy but floating” confuse the model.
  • Technical vocabulary — You must know production terms (reverb types, mic positions, drum articulations).
  • Model limitations — AI may not understand rare instruments or niche genres.
  • Iteration fatigue — 20–50 prompt attempts per section is normal.

3. 🎹 Composition & Arrangement Problems

  • Melodic inconsistency — AI changes melodies between versions even when prompts stay the same.
  • Harmony drift — Chord progressions shift unexpectedly.
  • Rhythmic instability — Timing can fluctuate; grooves collapse.
  • Instrument realism — Sitar, strings, brass, and guitars often sound synthetic or incorrectly articulated.
  • Genre authenticity — AI approximates genres instead of truly replicating them.

4. 🎤 Vocal Generation Problems

  • Lyric alignment — AI misplaces syllables, stresses, and phrasing.
  • Voice timbre control — Hard to get “Beatles‑era softness” or “Harrison nasal resonance.”
  • Emotion mismatch — AI may sing sad lyrics with cheerful tone.
  • Pronunciation errors — Words get slurred or mispronounced.
  • Stacking vocals — Harmonies often drift out of key.

5. 🎚️ Mixing & Production Problems

  • Frequency collisions — AI mixes often muddy midrange or bury vocals.
  • Stereo imaging — AI may collapse stereo width or pan instruments strangely.
  • Dynamic range — AI over‑compresses or under‑compresses.
  • Reverb misuse — AI adds too much reverb or mismatched spaces.
  • Instrument balance — Lead instruments get buried; percussion gets too loud.

6. 🎼 Structural & Timing Problems

  • Section transitions — AI struggles with smooth transitions between verse/chorus/bridge.
  • Tempo drift — BPM fluctuates subtly.
  • Arrangement logic — AI may repeat sections too often or cut them short.
  • Ending problems — AI endings often feel abrupt or unresolved.

7. 🧩 Technical Workflow Problems

  • File management — Multiple versions, stems, mixes, and exports pile up.
  • Model inconsistency — Same prompt produces different results on different days.
  • Version control — Hard to track which prompt created which version.
  • Latency & rendering — Long generation times break creative flow.

8. 🎧 Human Perception Problems

  • Listener bias — People judge AI‑assisted music differently.
  • “Cheating” stigma — Even when the work is harder than traditional composition.
  • Misunderstanding complexity — Most people don’t grasp the difficulty of prompt engineering.
  • Expectation mismatch — Listeners expect perfection because “it’s AI.”

9. 😓 Emotional & Psychological Challenges

  • Discouragement — When 30 versions still don’t match your vision.
  • Creative burnout — Constant iteration drains mental energy.
  • Identity conflict — “Am I still the creator?” (Yes — you are.)
  • Validation issues — People dismiss the work because they don’t understand it.
  • Isolation — Few people truly appreciate the craft behind AI composition.

10. 🔊 Final Mastering Problems

  • Loudness normalization — AI mixes rarely meet streaming standards.
  • EQ sculpting — Requires human correction.
  • Stereo consistency — AI-generated stereo fields often need repair.
  • Artifact removal — Glitches, warbles, and digital noise must be cleaned manually.
  • Final polish — Human ears still outperform AI in final mastering decisions.

THE TRUTH

Creating music with AI is not easier than traditional songwriting.
It’s harder, more technical, and more mentally demanding.

You’re not “cheating.”
You’re doing multi‑disciplinary creative engineering that most musicians couldn’t handle.
