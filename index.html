KINETIC DREAM

The Kinetic Dream — Origin Story

So, you've never heard of the Kinetic Dream? It's no wonder. This was a gathering of three young men who were all located in Huntsville, Alabama. They all had one thing in common: they loved rock music. Being southern rebels, they didn't want to just be a cover band. Murphy (who was called Bob at that time) and "Stewart" became music and song writers and came up with a folk/rock sound that was different than anything out at the time.

The year was 1981. MTV was fresh, and the music on the radio was evolving from mostly black urban and punk to mainstream pop. Murphy Gunnison was recently in the Navy and had gone absent without leave, unknown to the rest of the band, family, and friends. He was reaching out and grabbing at whatever came his way.

At some point he met Michael McAnally, the soon‑to‑be back‑beat drummer for the group, and they struck up a brotherly friendship that has withstood the test of time. Murphy had now found something he'd wanted as far back as he could remember: a friendship and an outlet for his music. He put his Navy troubles in a dark place in his mind and enjoyed what would become the best year of his life.

Mike McAnally was a mellow guy with a great sense of humor and rhythm. He was nobody's fool—he could smell trouble from a distance and steered clear of it whenever possible. He was, and is, an extremely talented drummer and guitar player. On drums, he could pattern his style after Rush's Neil Peart, the Beatles' Ringo Starr, and others with amazing precision. His demeanor could literally turn a funeral into a party.

Donald Stewart was his momma and daddy's son—loving and watchful over them. It's unclear whether the group found him or he found the group, but once he arrived, the lineup was complete. He had a quirky voice (in a good way) that fit perfectly and made the sound captivating.

At this point the group was:

  • Stewart — lead vocals, rhythm guitar
  • Bob — backing vocals, rhythm guitar, some lead guitar, bass guitar
  • Mike — drums, backing vocals, rhythm guitar
  • Jeff Kendrick — occasional bass guitar

They played a couple of parties and a farm concert in the middle of nowhere. Not much of a tour, but it was theirs. They even traveled to Nashville to try to impress anyone on Music Row—and they kind of did.

They brought a postal copyright copy of their original music to American Sound Records, a modest studio next door to Capitol Records where Paul McCartney was rumored to have visited. They proudly handed over their tape. The engineer unwrapped it, spooled it onto his reel‑to‑reel, and hit play.

It wasn’t them.

It was Don’s father, playing a slightly out‑of‑tune guitar and singing old country songs. Worse, the tape had been recorded in eight separate tracks—four forward and four reverse—all playing at the same time. A complete train wreck.

The engineer, kind but honest, told them they seemed like fine young men and should bring back the right tape. But the moment crushed them. It was the beginning of the end.

Shortly after, the Navy put out word they were trying to locate “Bob.” When Stewart found out, he punched Bob in the nose. Bob returned to the Navy and turned himself in. Thanks to his testimony and a key military witness, the AWOL charge was dropped, and he was honorably discharged.

Now, 36 years later, life has happened—and opportunity knocks again. Where will this road lead?

*Postal copyright: Record your music on tape, seal and mail it to yourself. The postmark proves the work existed before that date.
